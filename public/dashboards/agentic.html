01

Own sturdy businesses with agent optionality, not just the story

All seven names share one setup: real, growing, publicly traded businesses trading well off their highs, each with an optional agent-economy catalyst layered on top of an existing operation. That is different from buying a story with no revenue behind it — but it also means the size of the drawdown reflects real, non-thematic risks (credit, regulation, competition) as much as it reflects the market ignoring the thesis. Segmenting by how much of the investment case already exists today vs. depends on the thesis separates three genuinely different bets.

The honest framing: these seven are positively correlated on the macro question ("does the agent economy show up on schedule?"). They are not seven independent ideas — they are one idea expressed seven ways, at different points on the real-business-vs-optionality spectrum. Diversification against being wrong has to come from outside this basket, not within it.

02

The listings came first; the volume is arriving now

The setup is sequential, not simultaneous. Two of the seven went public in 2025 near a sentiment peak and were subsequently crushed; the volume and regulatory evidence that might justify the thesis mostly shows up afterward, in 2026 — which is exactly the gap between "priced" and "proven" this dashboard is built to track.

None of this is capacity or revenue in dispute — USDC circulation, CoreWeave's backlog and the x402 launch are real, reported numbers. What is unproven is the connective tissue: whether "agent compute" commands a durable price premium, whether stablecoin rails specifically (not just crypto generally) capture agent-to-agent payment volume, and whether a neobank's data advantage survives contact with a user's own AI agent shopping for a better deal.

2025
Two IPOs set the stage. Circle lists on the NYSE (June) as the first regulated public stablecoin issuer; CoreWeave lists (March) as the first pure-play neocloud. Both now trade 27–76% below their highs going into 2026.
Q1 2026
The volume numbers turn up first. USDC circulation reaches $77B (+28% YoY), on-chain settlement volume hits $21.5T (+263% YoY), and total stablecoin supply reaches $273B; CoreWeave's backlog hits $66B even as near-term revenue guidance disappoints the market.
May 2026
The payment rail and the regulation arrive together. Coinbase's x402 protocol (HTTP-native stablecoin micropayments) goes live with Amazon, Stripe and Cloudflare adopting it; the GENIUS Act sets KYC/AML requirements for stablecoin issuers, converting compliance into a moat for regulated incumbents.
June 2026
The card networks and the neobanks start moving. Mastercard launches Agent Pay and acquires BVNK for $1.8B; Visa's stablecoin settlement run-rate reaches $7B annualized; Nubank's CTO outlines an explicit AI-agent transformation strategy (June 11), and SoFi's Galileo unit ships Cyberbank Konecta AI agents into production.

06

Five assumptions, explicitly not consensus

The source note is upfront that these are extrapolations, not consensus views — that honesty is preserved here. Confidence below is my read of the evidence cited for each one; every position above depends on at least one of them being roughly right.

A1

Agentic inference outgrows hyperscaler capacity

Bursty, stateful, latency-sensitive agent workloads are different enough from training workloads that neoclouds can charge a 2–3x "agent compute premium" for guaranteed low-latency inference. CoreWeave's $66B backlog is the leading evidence.

ConfidenceModerate
What would update itA disclosed inference-vs-training revenue split showing premium pricing would confirm it. The disconfirmer: a hyperscaler shipping a dedicated low-latency inference SLA at standard pricing.
A2

Stablecoin rails are the only rational agent-payment choice

Agent-to-agent micropayments (often sub-$0.01) make 2.9%+$0.30 card economics nonsensical, so stablecoin rails (x402, USDC) capture the volume by default. The volume figures cited ($21.5T on-chain, $273B supply) are real; the causal link to "agent-driven" specifically is not yet isolated.

ConfidenceModerate
What would update itCoinbase, Stripe or Circle disclosing what share of x402/USDC volume is agent-initiated (vs. human crypto activity) would sharpen this considerably in either direction.
A3

Neobanks become "agent operating systems"

A neobank's identity, transaction and credit data make it the natural place a personal AI agent plugs in, creating switching costs far beyond a normal bank switch. Real evidence so far is limited to strategy announcements (Nubank, SoFi), not a shipped consumer product.

ConfidenceModerate-low
What would update itA first consumer-facing agent-banking feature actually shipping (not just announced) would meaningfully raise confidence; continued announcement-only progress through 2027 would lower it.
A4

Regulation bifurcates the market toward regulated incumbents

The GENIUS Act and FATF stablecoin guidance push compliance costs that regulated issuers (Circle) can absorb and unregulated protocols cannot, making regulatory status a moat rather than a burden. This is the most grounded assumption — it follows a well-established regulatory pattern.

ConfidenceModerate-high
What would update itGENIUS Act final rules landing with workable (not per-transaction) KYC requirements would confirm it; onerous per-transaction verification requirements would break the CRCL thesis specifically.
A5

Compute and payments converge into one "agent infrastructure cloud"

A neocloud that bundles GPU capacity with integrated stablecoin payment rails creates a closed loop stickier than either a pure-compute or pure-payments company. The source note itself is explicit that no company has announced this yet — Cloudflare Workers plus x402 is the closest live example.

ConfidenceLow
What would update itAny neocloud (CoreWeave or otherwise) announcing native payment-rail integration would be the first real evidence this assumption is playing out rather than purely speculative.
07

What breaks the whole framework

These are structural risks to the assumptions themselves, each paired with a tripwire from the source note — a specific, checkable event that should change position sizing, not just sentiment.

Agent adoption is a 10-year story, not 3

All seven theses assume meaningful adoption within 2026–2028. If consumers and enterprises resist delegating financial decisions to agents, every thesis here is early by years, not quarters. Tripwire: no major consumer agent-banking feature ships by Q2 2027 → cut NU/SOFI/HOOD exposure by half.

Stablecoin regulation restricts rather than enables

The GENIUS Act could land with per-transaction KYC requirements onerous enough to erase the low-friction advantage stablecoin rails have over cards. Tripwire: final rules require per-transaction identity verification → the CRCL thesis breaks; exit.

Hyperscalers commoditize the neocloud

If AWS, Azure or GCP ship dedicated low-latency GPU inference tiers at standard pricing, the architectural edge behind the "agent compute premium" disappears. Tripwire: a hyperscaler announces a sub-50ms inference SLA → cut CRWV/IREN exposure by half.

One protocol disintermediates the infrastructure layer

If x402 (or a competitor) gets built natively into model providers (OpenAI, Anthropic) and cloud platforms, the payment infrastructure layer becomes invisible — USDC still wins as the settlement asset, but card rails get bypassed. Tripwire: a major AI lab ships native protocol support with no third-party infra → MQ thesis weakens.

This reads like seven ideas. It behaves like one.

The most important thing to get right about this basket isn't which name is best — it's recognizing that all seven move on the same underlying question. The pairs below compound each other's coverage of the stack; none of them are actually uncorrelated with each other.

What this basket is

One correlated bet, expressed seven ways

If the core "does the agent economy show up on schedule" question resolves badly, most or all seven positions decline together — they share the same macro driver, not independent, uncorrelated theses. Owning all seven is concentration in disguise, not diversification.

Where the real diversification lives

Outside this basket, by design

The source note's own allocation discipline is worth keeping: size each position at roughly 2–5% of a dedicated thematic sleeve, and cap total agent-economy exposure at 15–25% of the overall portfolio. Because these seven are positively correlated with each other, genuine diversification has to come from non-thematic holdings — traditional equities, cash, or assets that don't share this specific macro driver — not from adding an eighth or ninth name to this list.

Treat the whole page as one position-sizing decision, not seven.
