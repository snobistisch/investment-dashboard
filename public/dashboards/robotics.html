Equity & token research overview · Q1 2026 reporting cycle

The robot gets the headline. The gearbox gets paid.

Physical AI — foundation models, simulation and cheap actuation converging on machines that move — is inflecting for reasons that hold up to scrutiny. But "robotics" is not a coherent thing to buy. Public exposure splits into three profiles that behave nothing alike: the humanoid pure-plays that own the story carry the most binary risk, while the best-capitalised names — component shovels and the compute layer — are only fractionally exposed to it. This dashboard maps that spread, equities and tokens.

01

Buy the theme through three different risk profiles, not one ticker

The demand signal is genuine and the capital following it is large — robotics start-ups have already raised more in 2026 than in all of 2025. What breaks is the instinct to treat it as a single trade. Once you segment by what actually pays you back, the universe separates into components and automation that earn regardless of which robot maker wins, a compute-and-platform layer betting on a physical-AI flywheel, and humanoid pure-plays that are option value on a still-unproven commercial model. Each behaves differently in a drawdown and rewards a different holding period.

The trade-off is the same one that governs every young hardware theme: the most investable, best-capitalised names are only partially exposed — a compute vendor or a strain-wave-gear maker gets a small slice of revenue from humanoids today. The purest plays are small, mostly loss-making, and swing on order flow and pilots that have not yet become fleets. How much theme-purity is worth paying for is the organising question here, not a footnote.

02

The inflection is a capability shift, not just a narrative

Three things changed at once. The software crossed a threshold, China turned humanoids into a manufacturing race, and — most important for public investors — capital stopped funding demos and started funding factories.

The technical base is now a stack, not a research project: a physical-AI platform (foundation model, synthetic-data pipeline, simulator and on-robot compute) that a maker can build on rather than assemble from scratch. China then did to humanoids what it did to EVs — drove cost down and volume up, producing the first genuinely low-priced units and a would-be industry leader. And the money followed a specific pattern: mega-cap capex on the compute layer, a marquee IPO window opening, and a large automaker converting a car line into a robot line. That is durable demand rather than a funding-round headline. The counter-signal is equally real — deployments are still pilots, not fleets, and a single supply chain in China controls the magnets that make every joint move.

2024 → 2025
Physical AI becomes a platform. NVIDIA's Isaac GR00T stack and Jetson Thor (2,070 FP4 TFLOPS on-robot) ship as a general foundation for robot builders; vision-language-action models move from papers to product, and the ambition is explicitly to be "the Android of generalist robotics."
2025
China turns humanoids into a manufacturing race. Unitree becomes the world's top humanoid seller, with humanoids jumping to 51.5% of revenue (from 1.9% in 2023) at a 62.9% gross margin; low-priced units and dense local supply chains reset the cost base.
Early → mid 2026
Capital funds factories, and the IPO window cracks open. Robotics start-ups raise ~$18.8B YTD — past all of 2025 — while NVIDIA ships Halos, a full-stack robot safety system, and Qualcomm and the analog semis formalise humanoid-specific product lines. Unitree wins approval for a ~$618M Shanghai STAR IPO at a ~$6.2B target valuation.
Through 2026
Build phase, not deployment phase. Tesla converts its Fremont Model S/X line to Optimus with production starting late summer (management calls 2026 output "impossible to predict," likely low thousands); the industry is entering factories and pilots but remains early, uneven and far from mass deployment.

06

Four competing hypotheses, held open on purpose

Not a stacked bull case. These genuinely compete; the right portfolio depends on which one you believe. Confidence is my read of current evidence — each pairs with the specific thing that would move it.

H1

Component shovels win

Reducers, actuators, magnets and sensors capture value regardless of which robot maker succeeds — the parts are on every bill of materials. You sidestep the humanoid binary and ride category-wide unit growth.

ConfidenceModerate
What would update itHumanoid order growth showing up in supplier backlogs at held margins would confirm it. The live disconfirmer: Harmonic Drive's operating profit fell 94% in FY2026 — proof that pricing power isn't automatic when Chinese component makers ramp.
H2

The platform layer compounds

As hardware commoditises, the durable edge is the physical-AI stack — foundation model, simulator, on-robot compute — or a proven autonomy platform with real deployments. A few players take most of the value.

ConfidenceModerate
What would update itRobotics becoming a material, separately-disclosed line for NVIDIA, or Symbotic converting its backlog into sustained GAAP profit, would confirm it. Against it: open models and Chinese full-stack makers could keep the platform premium thin.
H3

Humanoid pure-plays are option value

Most listed pure-plays are lottery tickets on a still-unproven commercial model; a few re-rate hard on catalysts. The profitable humanoid leader is offshore (Unitree), not a US-listed name.

ConfidenceHigh (as characterisation)
What would update itA humanoid reaching profitable, repeat fleet deployment — not just pilots — would upgrade the cohort from "option" to "franchise." Continued pilot-only traction and cash burn keep it as option value.
H4

Value accrues to incumbents

Cross-cutting bear case: the real winners are the automation incumbents and Big Tech — who own distribution, balance sheet and the compute layer — squeezing listed pure-plays between commoditising hardware and their own build-outs.

ConfidenceModerate
What would update itTesla shipping Optimus at volume, or an incumbent (Fanuc/ABB) fielding a credible humanoid, would confirm it. A pure-play reaching escape velocity before incumbents catch up — Unitree is the test — would refute it.
07

What breaks the whole thesis

These apply across every profile above, and matter most for Profile C. None is a reason to dismiss the theme — each is a reason to size and stage exposure carefully.

Deployment, not demos

Humanoids are in pilots, trade shows and pre-orders — not profitable fleets. Reliability, safety and total cost of ownership at scale are unproven. Until a humanoid works a real shift economically, the core value claim is a projection.

Capital intensity & dilution

Pure-plays burn cash for years — Serve guides ~$26M of revenue against $160–170M of opex — and fund themselves through equity. "Scaling fleet" can become a value trap if unit economics don't turn before the balance sheet does.

The rare-earth chokepoint

China makes ~94% of the NdFeB magnets every actuator needs and controls their export; a partial suspension of the strictest rules only runs to November 2026. The same concentration applies to reducers and motors — a supply-chain risk no software fixes.

Hype-cycle & rate sensitivity

A "$40 trillion TAM" framing and ETF inflows have pulled valuations forward. Long-duration, mostly-unprofitable robotics is acutely rate-sensitive, and narrative can compress multiples fast when a marquee milestone slips.

08

Two ways to own the machine economy — equities and tokens

There is a second, far more speculative way to express "robots need coordination, data and positioning": tokenised DePIN networks. A basket of PEAQ · GEOD · BitRobot looks nothing like an equity theme play — it is a crypto-infrastructure bet with tiny revenues, token-supply overhangs and mostly crypto-native sourcing. Same physical-AI tailwind, entirely different instrument and risk profile. Included for completeness, flagged as high-uncertainty.

Instrument A · Listed equities

Own the value chain through public markets

Optimises for durable exposure and investable quality — profitable component shovels and perception (6324, HSAI), a real autonomy platform (SYM) and the compute layer (NVDA), with humanoid pure-plays (Unitree, SERV) as the option-value sleeve. Auditable filings, regulated venues, lower single-name variance; you're betting the theme compounds over years.

This is what the six deep dives are built for.

Instrument B · Tokenised DePIN

Bet on crypto rails for the machine economy

Optimises for asymmetric upside in networks that pay machines and contributors in tokens. Much higher variance, thin liquidity, driven by adoption and token mechanics rather than earnings. Revenues are real but small; token unlocks and disclosure quality are material risks. Notably, peaq and Serve Robotics ran a live on-chain robot-payment demo — the clearest equity/token crossover so far.

09

Where to point due diligence next — mapped to your bet

Not a buy list and not a single recommendation — the honest output is which names to diligence depends on which hypothesis you're backing. Start here, then verify against primary filings.

If you back H1 · Shovels

Components first

6324 · HSAI · 6268
context: MP, CGNX

Diligence humanoid order backlogs and — for Harmonic — whether pricing power survives Chinese competition. Hesai is the highest-quality name here: a profitable perception business with a growing robotics line, not a pure bet.

If you back H2 · Platforms

Flywheel provers

SYM · NVDA · TSLA

Test whether the platform edge monetises. Symbotic is the only one with real robotics revenue converting to GAAP profit; NVIDIA is the arms dealer but immaterially exposed; Tesla is the incumbent optionality. Watch hardware-commoditisation risk.

If you back H3 · Option value

Catalyst & cash coverage

Unitree · SERV · 9880
event-driven, size small

Diligence the catalyst (IPO, fleet milestone), the cash runway through it, and the downside floor. Unitree is the only profitable one; treat these as staged, small, event-driven positions — not core holdings.

The one cross-cutting view I hold with reasonable confidence: separate the trade from the theme. Decide first whether you want durable thematic exposure through components and the platform layer, or asymmetric single-name bets on the humanoid catalyst — and whether you're expressing any of it in equities or tokens. Mixing them without deciding is how the drawdowns catch you off guard.
